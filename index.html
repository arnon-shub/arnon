Features

End-to-end encrypted

ECDH key exchange with AES-256-GCM. Every conversation generates fresh keys. A safety number lets both sides confirm no one is in the middle. Runs entirely in your browser via Web Crypto API.

No trace

Close the tab and everything is destroyed. Keys, messages, identity — all gone. Nothing saved anywhere.

No account

No phone number. No email. No registration. No identity. You're nobody — by design.

No download

Works in any browser. Phone, laptop, tablet. Nothing to install. Send a link, start chatting.

Text & voice

Send text messages and voice notes up to 30 seconds. Everything encrypted end-to-end. Nothing stored. No photos, videos, or file sharing — by design.

Self-destruct

Set a timer when you create a conversation. When time's up, the room is destroyed on both sides — even if nobody closes the tab.

Small enough to verify

The whole thing — app and relay combined — is about 600 lines. Small enough to read every line yourself in an afternoon, instead of trusting that someone audited a codebase you'll never open. All open source on GitHub, and the crypto runs in your browser.

Blind relay

Messages pass through a relay that holds only encrypted blobs in memory — nothing is written to disk. No accounts, no stored logs. Hosted in Helsinki, EU (GDPR).

Other private messengers store your account and history on a device or a server. Arnon runs entirely in your browser — no install, no sign-up, and nothing kept after you close the tab.

Arnon is a free, self-hosted side project. There is no company behind it raising money — just a single relay server and a browser app designed to disappear.

How to use Arnon, and what it protects

Arnon is built for conversations more private than WhatsApp or Telegram. Those platforms hold the conversation itself — your messages, voice notes, and who you talk to. Arnon holds none of it: the chat is end-to-end encrypted, nothing is stored, and nothing remains after you close the tab.

One thing Arnon can't control: how you send the link. Sharing it over a normal channel (a text, WhatsApp, email) lets that channel see that a link was shared — but never the conversation. So the worst case is far better than chatting on those apps directly: they'd see everything; here they'd only see that two people exchanged a link. For a personal chat, send the link through your usual app, then hold the actual conversation in Arnon — not in the app you sent it through.

The relay can never read your messages — they're encrypted end-to-end and the keys never leave the two browsers. It does see that two addresses are connected, when, and roughly how much. To hide that too, use Tor Browser.

Honest limit: because the app loads fresh in your browser each time, you trust whoever serves it. Against an adversary who can compromise the server, DNS, or TLS and target you specifically, an installed, independently verified client (Signal, SimpleX) is stronger. Arnon is built for low-friction, identity-free privacy — not for surviving a targeted server compromise.
